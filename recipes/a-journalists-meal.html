A Journalist's Meal

A Gift to Young Hosuewives is a classic Russian cookbook written and compiled by Elena Molokhovets. From 1861 to 1917--between the emancipation of the serfs and the start of the Bolshevik Revolution--Molokhovets continually revised the volume, becoming a household name in Russia through the decades. During the Soviet era, her famously over-the-top recipes were simultaneously condemned as a symbol of bourgeois decadence by Communist ideologues and envied by those waiting in bread lines. Today the book, known simply as "Molokhovets," is regarded as a historical record of traditional--if aristocratic--Russian cooking.

Anton Chekhov parodied her in an early sketch, The Alarm-Clock's Calendar, with a series of absurdly indulgent menus such as this one, an eight-course meal for journalists:

  1. A glass of vodka
  2. Daily shchi [cabbage soup] and yesterday's kasha
  3. Two glasses of vodka
  4. Suckling pig with horseradish
  5. Three glasses of vodka
  6. Horseradish, cayenne peper, and soy sauce
  7. Four glasses of vodka
  8. Seven bottles of beer

I do not recommend trying this at home unless you are a professional.
